\section{Conclusion}
\noindent The report demonstrated the design and testing of two different control approaches. Transfer functions relating motor voltage to arm and pendulum angles were systematically derived from linearised equations of motion, providing the foundation for subsequent controller design. A PID controller was developed using heuristic tuning and, yielded $T_s = 3.5$ s and $OS = 4.4\%$ during hardware simulation. The trial-and-error approach was deemed the most appropriate for the coupled dynamics of the pendulum and rotary arm.

\noindent A lead-lag compensator was designed using frequency-domain methods, producing the following metrics: $PM = 90°$ at $\omega_{PM} = 8.05$ rad/s and theoretical performance of $T_s = 3.53$ s and $OS = 4.72\%$. Simulink results showed $OS = 23.3\%$, illustrating the compromise that must be made between aggressive 
stability margins and realistic noise sensitivity.

\noindent It can be observed that the PID controller exhibits better practical robustness, with lower overshoot and a shorter settling time; thus, it is more suitable for hardware deployment.

\noindent This work underlines some of the basic compromises to be made in control system design: stability vs. response speed, noise rejection vs. bandwidth, and theoretical performance vs. practical implementability.
